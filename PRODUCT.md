# Product

## Register

product

## Users

GuideAnything serves three people working on the same ERP teaching material at different moments.

- **Authors** turn operational know-how into reusable visual guides. They spend sustained, focused time in a dense canvas and need confidence that their work is saved, versioned, and understandable.
- **Editors** make precise, permission-scoped changes to an author’s draft. They need unmistakable editability, state, and ownership without visual noise.
- **Learners** search published guidance and follow it step by step while performing a real ERP task. They need orientation, legibility, and a clear sense of progress, not authoring controls.

## Product Purpose

GuideAnything is a multi-modal ERP teaching workspace. It lets authors combine process maps, Markdown, images, video moments, and fixed-version sub-guides; learners then discover and follow published instructions in a focused reading flow.

The product succeeds when an unfamiliar employee can complete an ERP process with less uncertainty, and when a subject-matter expert can publish a trustworthy guide without wrestling with the tool. Every screen must make the current mode, next action, save state, and version boundary evident.

## Brand Personality

**Calm, precise, and quietly capable.**

The interface should feel like a carefully made native desktop tool: composed under pressure, comfortable during long sessions, and satisfying in small moments of progress. It takes inspiration from Apple’s clarity, layered materials, and adaptive appearance, without imitating Apple assets, product names, or platform chrome.

Dark and light appearances are equally first-class. Translucency protects the user’s sense of place in the canvas or lesson, but never becomes decoration that competes with teaching content.

## Anti-references

- Generic enterprise dashboards built from repeated cards, loud status colors, and metric-first hero panels.
- Dark “AI tool” aesthetics: black voids, neon purple or cyan gradients, glowing borders, shader backgrounds, and ornamental motion.
- Decorative glassmorphism: blur applied to every container, translucent text over uncontrolled imagery, and low-contrast frosted cards.
- Skeuomorphic Apple imitation, including copied icons, device frames, traffic-light controls, or faux system windows.
- Editorial serif display type used in authoring controls, data, menus, or step-by-step teaching content.

## Design Principles

1. **Keep the work in view.** Use material, blur, and elevation only to preserve spatial context while exposing controls or supporting information.
2. **Make state calm but unmistakable.** Draft, saved, publishing, published, selected, and error states must be discernible without relying on color alone.
3. **Make dense work feel ordered.** The editor may be information-dense, but grouping, spacing, and progressive disclosure must keep the active task readable.
4. **One vocabulary across modes.** Login, library, editor, and lesson views share the same semantic colors, shapes, focus treatment, and interaction feedback while adapting their density to the task.
5. **Adapt without surprise.** Follow the user’s light/dark preference by default, honor reduced motion and increased contrast, and preserve familiar web interaction patterns.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Meet WCAG 2.2 AA as the shipping baseline in both light and dark appearances. Maintain visible keyboard focus, 44px minimum touch targets for primary touch controls, semantic labels, and non-color cues for status and selection.

Use `prefers-reduced-motion` to remove nonessential material transitions and animated backgrounds. Test all translucent surfaces over their actual canvas, media, and content backdrops; when contrast is uncertain, increase material opacity or use an opaque fallback. Preserve the project’s existing keyboard shortcuts and screen-reader names as the visual system changes.
